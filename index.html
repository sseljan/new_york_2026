MANHATTAN - NBA FINALS - 2026

The Promise of Witness

Making sense of a team uniting a city on the night of the miraculous comeback of Game 4.

By Sam Seljan

We stepped out of the Japanese restaurant into the dark sauna of Manhattan. Garbage, diesel, weed. A warm wind off the Hudson. Yash and I joined the current of people moving toward the game, toward bars and windows and mounted TVs, toward screens that, for once, were not shrinking everyone into solitude but gathering them into public feeling.

The city was alive. Tough, skeptical, secular New York was alive in 2026, with another war in the Middle East, with AI swallowing software engineering and threatening to swallow work itself, with everyone half-dazed by the future and still somehow available to the tribal absurdity of a basketball team. Men wearing uniforms competing against each other had given New Yorkers something to care about. Together.

The further we walked, the thicker the stream of people and odors - clumps of friendship, wave after wave, every kind of New Yorker. First a group of middle-aged men laughing loudly: "Forget about it." Then 30 somethings wearing their 1990s polos and slacks. Then several large groups of Black and Latinos, displaying their Knicks fandom, if at all, with older hats or t-shirts.

They were all drawn toward those screens and alcohol and the promise of witness. A game. Just a game. But the NBA Finals, and so we knew - without needing to say it - that something was happening. We wanted to be there to see it, hear it, smell it. Not only the game, maybe not even mainly the game, but other people wanting what we wanted.

Yash and I worked together at AppNexus. We formed an unlikely friendship - me in Portland, a political scientist; him in New Jersey, a cognitive scientist from India and an avid cricket player - both building algorithms about advertising. I managed him through the trauma of the pandemic. All of us were confused and stunned, but still working, video conferencing, marveling at how fast everything can change, a primer for the coming onslaught of AI. He went to be with his family in India. When AT&T learned that employees had done this, I had to cover for him.

Then there was the trauma of being acquired by Microsoft after AT&T's strategy of building a modern media company blew up. Yash helped organize a conference for data scientists that was held in Burbank. He set up Yann LeCun as the guest speaker. The happy hour was held around the Batmobile from The Dark Knight. But it all went nowhere. Our dreams of contributing to HBO Max or Warner Bros. were smashed by the reality that linear TV was dying and AT&T had no idea how to run a media company.

"screens that, for once, were not shrinking everyone into solitude but gathering them into public feeling."

Yash and I always got along very well. Through this all we became close. Very close work friends. At the same time, we had not stayed in touch, and there were some bruised feelings from my decision to leave Microsoft.

We talked about old times and also watched the game, pausing at the points when the Knicks made a shot. We managed to find a spot with a clear view of the TV. Behind us was a group of true basketball fans, praising and dissing the big plays knowingly and confidently. Rookie Dylan Harper crosses over a defender and I hear: "Oh, that boy is criminal. Criminal." D'Aaron Fox throws the ball away lamely. A different voice: "Pfff, Fox, you garbage."

I turned to see who they were: a thin Black kid, maybe 25, with a ragged, sparse beard and sharp eyes, smirking. The other was his stout, jolly, confident friend in a Knicks hat. Our eyes meet. I smirk and agree: "Fox is a problem for the Spurs." We shared this tiny moment, agreeing on the aesthetics of the game. Was that what we were all there for? To share a moment together? Not with each other in particular, but with anyone who shared the sensibility that the NBA Finals with the Knicks was something worth caring about?

And why? I am not a Knicks fan. For me, I wanted to be there because the city cared about the team. Celebrities, people who could have anything, wanted this moment, and so did these kids. New York had become an underdog in basketball, but this team had a lovable grit and fire that inspired.

"New York is a city of elites but even more a city of underdogs, with outsized dreams."

The team was led by Jalen Brunson, a 6'2" point guard, tiny by NBA standards, who is crafty, rugged, fearless, and magical, floating shots over the arms of a man with nearly a 10-foot standing reach, Victor Wembanyama, off the backboard and into the hoop. He makes you think anything is possible. Brunson is David. Wembanyama is Goliath. There is a reason the story is in the Bible. New York is a city of elites but even more a city of underdogs, with outsized dreams. So Brunson is who they wish they could be - made alive through the poetic motion of sweat, swerve, and guile of this man, navigating among men who are skyscrapers. Literally a son of the city. If he can do this, we can do something.

But not tonight, it seems. The Knicks are down by 29 points after a frustrating loss in Game 3, the President's selfish presence complicating their big night, forcing watching parties away from the Garden, bringing out thousands of police officers in the streets. Every side street for 15 blocks barricaded with officers collected casually. Access to people's homes required showing ID. The flow of pedestrian traffic cut off on a whim. "You can't go this way... You just can't." That night, the people near the Garden were either looking for a fight or, like me, tourists watching other people. The police message was clear enough: you may have your worthless vote and the right to express yourself, but when it comes to something that matters, you ain't shit.

Tonight, away from the Garden in the Financial District, we linger to see if something could happen. That's why we love sports, right? Something could happen. And, at the simmering signs of a comeback, we cheer. The Knicks cannot hear us, but we cheer. After a few more baskets, we cheer louder. "Let's go Knicks! Let's go Knicks!" Of course we cheer. We are not cheering for Knicks basketball players; we are cheering for ourselves and each other to believe. To believe in something. In this one precious life you must believe. In something. Even if it is the triviality of a game of basketball among people you have never met, you must believe.

"We are not cheering for Knicks basketball players; we are cheering for ourselves and each other to believe."

And so we do. Yash and I stay and watch and continue our conversation. Yash takes the time to get something off his chest: my manager, Tanu, after I left, had shared with him a negative review that I gave her. He softens the moment by prefacing the story with: "I would never think badly of you because you are a friend and I love you." But it is on his permanent record. I wobble, ashamed, fighting an awkward smile, searching for the right words. Searching for what I had said and in what context so I can explain it away and blame her, knowing that was true and a lie at the same time. Reeling, I ask questions: "What did she show you?" The answer gives me enough time to compose myself. I both apologize and blame Tanu. I instantly wish I could have been stronger and more direct: "Yash, I am so sorry. I messed up." Something like that is what I should have said.

By the morning, my regret has dissolved, and what is left is the connection that was there between us, the recollection and satisfaction of the lasting value of doing my best as a manager and a colleague, even if it was imperfect. In those moments where, like everyone else, I wonder, "What the hell have I done with my life?" a connection like that is... the quiet of a redwood stand, a moment of brief tranquility when the overwhelming fact of the fundamental goodness of the universe and my place in it interrupts the chaotic stream of thoughts, feelings, burdens, and striving.

I leave at the end of the third quarter. I need to catch a flight out of New York. We walk back toward my hotel. We marvel at how strange it is to use AI to solve all our problems - how his decision to be a manager, so scorned by Tanu, has given him tools useful in writing clear instructions for AI, agentic engineering requires managerial clarity and even empathy. He grabs his bag from the hotel staff. A hug goodbye. "Let's keep in better touch," he says earnestly.

And the Knicks. What about the Knicks? They could not come back from 29 points. But they do come back. Yash hears the roar of the crowd just before getting to his train, stops in a bar, and gets to witness the magic. I learn about it in a text in the morning. He shares the ecstatic moment with strangers in a bar, texts me the video, and as the game-winning shot goes in, arms fly into the air, heads bob, friends and strangers hug, screams of joy...

"the improbable is paradoxically probable. This is the simple math of the universe."

Moments like these, shared together, remind us that in this life the improbable is paradoxically probable. This is the simple math of the universe. All of us being here now is close to impossible: an infinite number of events, moments, particles, waves, and vibrations in spacetime had to happen in just one of an infinite number of ways for any of us to exist, and yet here we all are. Making, unmaking, and remaking friendship. Hoping for no good reason that your team beats their team. For what feels like both forever and never, we are here - you might as well believe that we are meant to be here with each other and that our lives are extraordinary.
