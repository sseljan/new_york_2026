We stepped out of the Japanese restaurant into the dark sauna of Manhattan. Garbage, diesel, weed. A warm wind off the Hudson. Yash and I joined the current of people moving toward the game, toward bars and windows and mounted TVs, toward screens that, for once, were not shrinking everyone into solitude but gathering them into public feeling.

The city was alive. Tough, skeptical, secular New York was alive in 2026, with another war in the Middle East, with AI swallowing software engineering and threatening to swallow work itself, with everyone half-dazed by the future and still somehow available to the tribal absurdity of a basketball team. Men wearing uniforms competing against each other — a vestige of another era — had given New Yorkers something to care about. Together.

The further we walked, the thicker the stream of people and odors — clumps of friendship, wave after wave, every kind of New Yorker. First a group of middle-aged men laughing loudly: “Forget about it.” Then 30 somethings wearing their 1990s polos and slacks. Then several large groups of Black and Latinos, displaying their Knicks fandom, if at all, with older hats or t-shirts.

They were all drawn toward those screens and alcohol and the promise of witness. A game. Just a game. But the NBA Finals, and so we knew — without needing to say it — that something was happening. We wanted to be there to see it, hear it, smell it. Not only the game, maybe not even mainly the game, but other people wanting what we wanted.

Yash was with me because, a few days earlier, I had called him. I was going to be in the city, and on impulse I asked if he wanted to watch a Finals game. He said yes before I finished the sentence. We had worked together at AppNexus — an unlikely friendship, me in Portland, a political scientist; him in New Jersey, a cognitive scientist from India and an avid cricket player — both building algorithms about advertising. I managed him through the trauma of the pandemic, when all of us were confused and stunned but still working, video conferencing from our scattered rooms, marveling at how fast everything could change — a quiet primer for the onslaught of AI still to come. When the lockdowns hit, Yash flew home to his family in India; later, when AT&T discovered that employees had quietly left the country, I covered for him, earning his trust. We talked often about how to maintain a sense of normalcy in the chaos, stumbling our way through it together.

The hard things kept pulling us closer. After AT&T’s plan to build a modern media company blew up, we lived through the slow unraveling of the Microsoft acquisition together. Before it all came apart, there had been real ambition: Yash organized a data-science conference in Burbank and landed Yann LeCun as the guest speaker, the happy hour spilling around the Batmobile from The Dark Knight. But it went nowhere. Our dreams of contributing to HBO Max or Warner Bros. were smashed by the reality that linear TV was dying and AT&T had no idea how to run a media company.

And yet, for all that closeness, we hadn’t really stayed in touch. There were bruised feelings about my decision to leave Microsoft, and somewhere in the churn we’d let the friendship go quiet.

So there was plenty unspoken between us as we shouldered into the crowd and found a spot with a clear view of the TV. We talked about old times, pausing whenever the Knicks scored. Behind us stood a pair of friends focused on the basketball game, praising and dissing the big plays with easy confidence. Rookie Dylan Harper crossed over a defender and I heard: “Oh, that boy is criminal. Criminal.” D’Aaron Fox throws the ball away lamely. A different voice: “Pfff, Fox, you garbage.”

I turned to see who they were: a thin Black kid, maybe 25, with a ragged, sparse beard and sharp eyes, smirking. The other was his stout, jolly, confident friend in a Knicks hat. Our eyes meet. I smirk and agree: “Fox is a problem for the Spurs.” We shared this tiny moment, agreeing on the aesthetics of the game. Was that what we were all there for? To share a moment together? Not with each other in particular, but with anyone who shared the sensibility that the NBA Finals with the Knicks was something worth caring about?

And why? I am not a Knicks fan. For me, I wanted to be there because the city cared about the team. Celebrities, people who could have anything, wanted this moment, and so did these kids. That was the strange democracy of it — the famous and the broke, briefly wanting the same thing.

They wanted it for many reasons. Certainly it mattered that it had been 53 years since they won the title. It mattered more that it was their type of team. Viewed from the heartland, New York reads as a place of privilege; on the streets you see it is a city of underdogs, everyday people with outsized dreams. The Knicks had given that feeling a body, and the body was Jalen Brunson — a 6'2" point guard, tiny by NBA standards, crafty, rugged, and somehow magical. New Yorkers love him. He is who they wish they could be, their dreams made vivid in his sweat, swerve, and guile, in the way he navigates fearlessly among men who are skyscrapers. If he can lead this team to a championship, they can do something too.

In this game, he floats a shot over Victor Wembanyama — over the arms of a man with nearly a 10-foot standing reach — banking it off the backboard and into the hoop. Brunson is David. Wembanyama is Goliath. There is a reason that story is in the Bible.

But tonight isn't his night. The Knicks are down by 29 points. The season is going the wrong way. In Game 3, the President’s selfish presence complicated their big night, forcing watching parties away from the Garden, bringing out thousands of police officers in the streets. Every side street for 15 blocks barricaded with officers collected casually. Access to people’s homes required showing ID. The flow of pedestrian traffic cut off on a whim. “You can’t go this way... You just can’t.” That night, the people near the Garden were either looking for a fight or, like me, tourists people-watching. The police message was clear enough: you may have your vote and the right to express yourself, but when it comes to something that matters, you ain’t shit.

Tonight, away from the Garden in the Financial District, we linger to see if something could happen. That’s why we love sports, right? Something could happen. And, at the simmering signs of a comeback, we cheer. The Knicks cannot hear us, but we cheer. After a few more baskets, we cheer louder. “Let’s go Knicks! Let’s go Knicks!” Of course we cheer. We are not cheering for Knicks basketball players; we are cheering for ourselves and each other to believe. To believe in something. In this one precious life you must believe. In something. Even if it’s the triviality of a game of basketball among people you have never met, you must believe.

Yash and I stay and watch and continue our conversation. Yash takes the time to get something off his chest: my manager, Tanu, after I left, had shared with him a negative review that I gave her. He softens the moment by prefacing the story with: “I would never think badly of you because you are a friend and I love you.” But it’s on his permanent record. I wobble, ashamed, fighting an awkward smile, searching for the right words. Searching for what I had said and in what context so I can explain it away and blame her, knowing that was true and a lie at the same time. Reeling, I ask questions: “What did she show you?” The answer gives me enough time to compose myself. I both apologize and blame Tanu. I instantly wish I could have been stronger and more direct: “Yash, I am so sorry. I messed up.” Something like that is what I should have said.

By the morning, my regret has dissolved, and what’s left is the connection that was there between us, the recollection and satisfaction of the lasting value of doing my best as a manager and a colleague, even if it was imperfect. In those moments where, like everyone else, I wonder, “What the hell have I done with my life?” a connection like that is... the quiet of a redwood stand, a moment of brief tranquility when the overwhelming fact of the fundamental goodness of the universe and my place in it interrupts the chaotic stream of thoughts, feelings, burdens, and striving.

I leave at the end of the third quarter. I need to catch a flight out of New York. We walk back toward my hotel. We marvel at how strange it is to use AI to solve all our problems — how his decision to be a manager, so scorned by Tanu, has given him tools useful in writing clear instructions for AI, and how agentic engineering — the term of the moment for using AI to code — requires managerial clarity and even empathy... Yash grabs his bag from the hotel staff. A hug goodbye. “Let’s keep in better touch,” he says earnestly.

And the Knicks. What about the Knicks? They couldn’t come back from 29 points. But they do. Yash hears the roar of the crowd just before getting to his train, stops in a bar, and gets to witness the magic. I learn about it in a text in the morning. He shares the ecstatic moment with strangers in a bar, texts me the video, and as the game-winning shot goes in, arms fly into the air, heads bob, friends and strangers hug, screams of joy.

Moments like these, shared together, remind us that in this life the improbable is paradoxically probable. This fact is the simple math at the heart of our mysterious universe. All of us being here now is nearly impossible: an infinite number of events had to happen in just one of an infinite number of ways for any of us to exist, and yet here we all are, making, unmaking, and remaking friendship. Hoping for no good reason that our team beats their team. For what feels like both forever and never, we are here — so we might as well believe that we are meant to be here with each other.
